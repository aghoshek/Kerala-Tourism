• Thiruvananthapuram

Situated at the southernmost point of India's western coast, Thiruvananthapuram, also known as Trivandrum, lies nestled between the serene Arabian Sea to the west and the vibrant state of Tamil Nadu to the east. Its name, derived from "Anantha Padmanabha Swamy," an incarnation of Lord Vishnu, aptly reflects the city's rich cultural heritage, evident in its numerous ancient temples.read more..

• Kollam

Once a bustling hub of international trade, Kollam, formerly known as Quilon, played a pivotal role in the spice trade, exporting its rich bounty of black pepper, cinnamon, and cloves to far-flung corners of the globe. Renowned for its exquisite cashew nuts, Kollam remains a vital center for cashew processing and trade, upholding its legacy as a haven for aromatic treasures.read more..

• Pathanamthitta

Nestled amidst the verdant foothills of the Western Ghats, Pathanamthitta, fondly known as the "Pilgrim's Paradise," stands as a haven of tranquility and spiritual rejuvenation. Its lush landscapes, encompassing lowlands, midlands, and highlands, are traversed by three meandering rivers, creating a tapestry of natural beauty and ecological diversity.read more..

• Alappuzha

Affectionately known as the "Venice of the East," Alappuzha, a district in Kerala, India, is a mesmerizing tapestry of tranquil backwaters, vibrant wildlife, and rich cultural heritage. Nestled between the Arabian Sea to the west and a labyrinth of interconnected lagoons, lakes, and freshwater rivers, Alappuzha unveils a serene paradise where nature's beauty unfolds at a leisurely pace.read more..

• Kottayam

Nestled amidst the picturesque backwaters and the towering Western Ghats, Kottayam stands as a vibrant hub of literary tradition, flourishing spice trade, and captivating natural beauty. Its fertile lands, adorned with vast expanses of greenery, verdant paddy fields, and sprawling rubber plantations, epitomize the district's rich agricultural heritage. read more..

• Idukii

Idukki, Kerala's second largest district, stands as a breathtaking tapestry of towering hill ranges, verdant valleys, and life-giving rivers. Nestled amidst the Western Ghats, this landlocked region is home to an abundance of natural wonders, including the Periyar, Thalayar, and Thodupuzhayar rivers and their tributaries, which nourish the land and provide a haven for diverse wildlife.read more..

• Ernakulam

Ernakulam, the bustling heart of Kerala, is a mesmerizing blend of urban energy, coastal charm, and economic prowess. Nestled on the shores of the Arabian Sea, it is a dynamic metropolis that seamlessly intertwines the allure of nature with the vibrancy of a thriving commercial hub.read more..

• Thrissur

Thrissur, the vibrant cultural capital of Kerala, stands as a captivating blend of time-honored traditions, awe-inspiring architecture, and a rich literary heritage. Its annual Pooram festivals, a kaleidoscope of colorful processions, rhythmic percussion, and fervent devotion, draw in thousands of people each year, transforming the city into a pulsating hub of cultural celebration.read more..

• Palakkad

Nestled at the foothills of the majestic Western Ghats, Palakkad, fondly known as the "Granary of Kerala," unveils a breathtaking tapestry of fertile plains, verdant hills, meandering rivers, and serene backwaters. A gateway to Kerala from the north, Palakkad is defined by its unique geographical feature, the Palakkad Gap, a 40-kilometer break in the mountains that provides access to this land of natural abundance.read more..

• Malappuram

Malappuram, a district in Kerala, India, stands as a captivating blend of ancient heritage, modern vibrancy, and natural splendor. Once the military headquarters of the powerful Zamorins of Kozhikode, Malappuram played a pivotal role in shaping the region's history, witnessing the rise and fall of empires and the struggle for freedom from colonial rule. The district was the epicenter of the Mappila Revolts, a series of uprisings against the British East India Company between 1792 and 1921, showcasing the indomitable spirit of its people.read more..

• Kozhikode

Nestled along the Malabar Coast of Kerala, Kozhikode, once known as Calicut, stands as a captivating blend of rich maritime history, vibrant cultural tapestry, and the rhythmic allure of the Arabian Sea. Once a bustling hub of international trade, Kozhikode attracted merchants from far-flung corners of the globe, including Arab, Chinese, and East African traders, who exchanged their precious goods and cultural influences along its shores.read more..

• Wayanad

Nestled amidst the verdant hills of Kerala's northernmost tip, Wayanad stands as a sanctuary of pristine wilderness, a haven where nature's beauty remains untouched and unspoiled. Renowned for its rich biodiversity, Wayanad is home to a treasure trove of rare flora and fauna, earning it the well-deserved title of one of Kerala's most picturesque destinations.read more..

• Kannur

Nestled along the northern coast of Kerala, Kannur, formerly known as Cannanore, stands as a captivating blend of ancient history, vibrant spice trade legacy, and the mesmerizing allure of traditional Theyyam performances. Once hailed by Marco Polo as a "Great Emporium of Spice Trade," Kannur played a pivotal role in shaping the region's commercial landscape, its ports bustling with merchants from far-flung corners of the globe, exchanging their precious spices and cultural influences.read more..

• Kasargod

Nestled amidst the enchanting landscapes of Kerala's northernmost district, Kasaragod stands as a captivating blend of natural beauty, rich cultural heritage, and flourishing craftsmanship. Its rolling hills, adorned with lush greenery and dotted with ancient forts, provide a breathtaking backdrop for nature enthusiasts and adventure seekers alike. The serene waters of its rivers and lakes, meandering through the verdant landscape, add a touch of tranquility to the surroundings.read more..
